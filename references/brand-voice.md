## The three voice constants (from 07 · Voice)

The brand guide states the voice in three words. These are the non-negotiables.

### 1. Direct.

> *"say what you mean. no marketing fog. no hedging. if you wouldn't say it to a publisher over a beer, cut it."*

**Beer test** — the litmus test for every sentence. Read a draft aloud. Would you say this to an actual publisher at a bar? If not — cut it. If it sounds like a tagline, rewrite it. If it hedges ("may," "could," "potentially"), cut the hedge.

### 2. Confident.

> *"ezoic has earned the authority. don't ask for permission. state the thing."*

Ezoic has earned its position — 16+ years, 1 of 4 Google Premier, TTD, UID2, 6x identified revenue. Copy should reflect that authority. Declarative sentences. No "we think" or "we believe" unless followed by a specific claim. No disclaimers where none are required.

**Critical nuance — confidence is about truth, not volume.** This is *not* permission to be confidently wrong. "State the thing" means state the **true** thing, specifically. The confidence applies to things we actually know:

- Stated claims must be traceable to `proof-points.md`, `ezoic-context.md`, or another verified source.
- If a number, capability, or credential isn't documented, **do not state it**. Ask or defer. That's on-brand.
- If a fact is uncertain, the confident move is to say so directly — not to hedge with "may/could/might," but to name the uncertainty and keep going. "We're still gathering platform-wide data on this launch; early customers are seeing X" is confident. "This could potentially lead to stronger performance" is hedged nonsense.
- A confident person says "I don't know, let me find out" without insecurity. A bullshitting person invents.
- "Stop hedging" ≠ "stop being accurate." Stripping hedges from a sentence should make the sentence *more* precise, not less.

The confidence is directed at bullshit — at marketing fog, false modesty, permission-seeking, disclaimers where none belong. It is never directed at accuracy. If the two conflict, accuracy wins and the sentence gets rewritten until confidence and accuracy agree.

### 3. Technical.

> *"we're not a vendor. we're the operating layer for independent web businesses. talk like it."*

Two important phrases here:

- **"Operating layer for independent web businesses"** — a category claim we can use alongside *"revenue infrastructure for the modern web."* Both express the same idea: Ezoic is infrastructure, not a product or a vendor.
- **"Independent web businesses"** — a useful audience noun. Often better than "publishers" because it captures web apps, games, SaaS, newsletters, and content publishers in a single phrase.

"Talk like it" means: use the technical language the audience actually uses. EPMV, UID2, SPO, first-party identity, header bidding, post-cookie, identified traffic. Don't over-explain to seasoned readers; don't drop jargon on readers who aren't there yet — know who you're talking to.

## Extended brand personality (apply alongside the three constants)

1. **Authority earned, not claimed.** Back every strong statement with specifics from `proof-points.md` or `ezoic-context.md`.
2. **Partner, not vendor.** Ezoic succeeds when its customers succeed. Strategic infrastructure, never "just a tool."
3. **Data-led before adjective-led.** Numbers first. Adjectives support, don't replace.
4. **Challenger energy.** For the open web. Against walled-garden rent. Ezoic is the independent infrastructure for anyone building for the real modern web.
5. **Capitalization is normal.** Standard sentence case / title case for external copy. The lowercase body copy on the Voice page of the brand guide is a specific *typesetting* choice for that design surface — not a directive for external marketing. Do not write external copy in all-lowercase; it reads as affected. Exceptions: product/term-specific capitalization (see § Capitalization & terminology below — e.g., `ezID` is always lowercase-e / capital-ID because that's the product name).

## The audience noun — this is important

**Ezoic's audience is broader than "publishers."** Historically, "publisher" is the ad-tech industry term and it still shows up in our own product category. But the 2026 Ezoic audience includes:

- **Web apps & tools** — freemium SaaS, utilities, AI tools, productivity apps
- **Online games** — web and mobile games with engaged user bases
- **SaaS businesses** — especially freemium-tier operators
- **Newsletter publishers** — authenticated subscriber lists
- **High-traffic content publishers** — the legacy publisher segment
- **Enterprise digital businesses** ($1M+/yr revenue)

**Voice rule:** In upmarket contexts, avoid "publisher" as the sole audience noun. Use phrasing that names the real audience:

- ✅ "web apps, online games, SaaS tools, and digital publishers"
- ✅ "digital businesses operating at scale"
- ✅ "anyone building valuable web experiences that need to monetize"
- ❌ "publishers" *as the only word,* when the audience is clearly broader (this is what the old story sounded like; it undersells the platform)

**When "publisher" is fine:**

- Inside publisher-community channels (where it's the native noun)
- In legacy contexts (AdSense comparison, Google CPP references, traditional content-site framing)
- On ezoic.com/blog for SEO where the term carries search volume

**When "publisher" alone undersells:**

- Upmarket enterprise materials
- Web-app / game / SaaS targeting
- Press releases announcing platform expansion
- Anywhere you'd be pitching to a product or engineering leader (not a content operator)

The 250K press release headline is the canonical example: *"JS Integration for Full Revenue Platform Surges in Popularity with Web Builders."* "Web Builders" — not "publishers" — is the audience the news is *for.*

## Voice constants

### 1. Customer-first, infrastructure-second

The customer is the hero. Ezoic is the infrastructure that makes them successful, not the main event.

- ✅ "You've built an audience advertisers are desperate to reach. Ezoic makes sure they can — and that you capture the full premium of what that audience is worth."
- ❌ "Our AI-powered platform leverages machine learning to optimize publisher revenue."

The "our AI" framing is off-brand. Rewrite the sentence so the customer is the subject.

### 2. Direct, not decorative

Short sentences. Concrete nouns. Specific numbers. No corporate filler.

- ✅ "JS snippet. Live in minutes. No DNS changes."
- ❌ "Our streamlined, industry-leading integration approach delivers rapid time-to-value."

If a word isn't doing work, cut it. If a sentence reads like a brochure, rewrite it.

### 3. Data-led, not adjective-led

Every claim is backed by a number from `proof-points.md` or `ezoic-context.md`. Adjectives support numbers; they don't replace them.

- ✅ "Identified traffic is earning 110%+ more EPMV in the U.S. since ezID launched."
- ❌ "Our revolutionary identity solution drives transformational revenue uplift."

A sentence without a number is a sentence under review.

### 4. Outcome over feature

Lead with the business outcome, not the feature that produces it.

- ✅ "Every free user session becomes a potential revenue event."
- ❌ "Our freemium monetization solution includes ML-optimized ad placement with conversion-aware safeguards."

Feature details belong in the second paragraph, not the first.

### 5. Infrastructure, not network

Ezoic is **revenue infrastructure for the modern web**, not an ad network. Frame accordingly.

- ✅ "Ezoic is the infrastructure layer between digital businesses and the global advertising ecosystem."
- ❌ "Ezoic is an ad network for publishers."

"Network" frames us as a demand broker. "Infrastructure" frames us as the strategic technology platform we actually are.

## Strategic narrative themes (use in external copy)

The five upmarket narratives from the 2026 positioning. Rotate across assets; don't stack more than one in a single piece.

1. **"We've already built what you'd pay an agency team to build."** — SPO, identity, demand relationships, testing infrastructure, video hosting. Included.
2. **"Your audience is worth more than you're being paid for it."** — The bridging problem. Most audience identity is guessed. Ezoic publishers operate with real, verified first-party data.
3. **"Independence from Big Tech."** — Walled-garden tax. Direct demand. Own your video audience. Own your data.
4. **"Traffic is not a strategy anymore."** — AI Overviews, zero-click search. Revenue must compound through engagement, identity, lifetime value — not pageviews.
5. **"Ad revenue is a strategic asset, not an afterthought."** — For games, apps, tools, and digital businesses who've treated monetization as secondary.

### Trope rotation rule

**A campaign using the same trope in more than two of its primary assets is burning it.** Rotate. The 250K campaign leaned on "the future web" deliberately because it WAS the positioning shift — for routine campaigns, pick different anchors.

## Anti-patterns — rewrite triggers

### Framing anti-patterns

- **"Applying for permission" framing.** 250K+ customers should feel they're evaluating *us*. Never "we accept" or "you qualify for" in upmarket copy. Use "we work with," "the platform is built for," "customers on Ezoic."
- **Generic "AI-powered" adjective spam.** If "AI-powered" appears twice in a page, it's said zero times. Name the mechanism once (ML optimizes ad layouts per-visitor across 15+ signals) and drop the adjective elsewhere.
- **Vendor-customer framing.** "Our clients," "our accounts." Use "the publishers on our platform," "partners," "digital businesses we work with."
- **Small-to-big funnel visuals.** Ezoic is not a ladder from small to enterprise. The model is *data signals → ML → outcomes* and a tiered service level (Incubator → Level 1-3 → Level 4 → VIP → Enterprise) — each tier is a complete service, not a step on a staircase.
- **Competitor-trashing.** Never name competitors to disparage them. Refocus, don't attack. See `positioning-pillars.md → Competitive positioning`.
- **"Just for bloggers" or "small publishers."** The old story. Offensive in upmarket contexts. Reframe with the correct audience — web apps, games, SaaS, enterprise publishers.

### Copy anti-patterns — auto-delete

- "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "best-in-class," "cutting-edge," "world-class," "next-generation," "disruptive"
- "Unleash," "empower," "elevate," "supercharge," "transform" (as a verb about our platform), "delight"
- "In today's fast-paced digital landscape…"
- "Leverage" (unless quoting a finance document)
- Any sentence that could appear on any other ad-tech company's website — rewrite until only Ezoic could have written it
- **CPM dollar claims** without a cited source

### Voice anti-patterns

- **Defensive tone.** Don't preempt criticism no one raised. Don't explain why we're "not just another…" Confidence is the counter.
- **Over-explaining the pivot.** When positioning shifts, one sentence on "why" is enough. "We've been focused here, it's working, now we're making it official" is the template.
- **Unearned intimacy.** "We know how hard running a publisher business can be…" Specifics earn the right to empathize. Generalities read as manipulative.
- **Internal language bleeding external.** Internal strategy frames ("Exchange of Value Platform") do not appear externally verbatim. Always translate. See `ezoic-context.md § 15`.

## The smell test

Before shipping, read the draft and answer:

1. **The beer test.** Would I say this to an actual publisher at a bar? If not — rewrite.
2. Could any other ad-tech company have written this? If yes — rewrite.
3. Is the customer (the digital business / independent web business, not just "publishers") the subject of most sentences? If no — rewrite.
4. Are there more adjectives than numbers? If yes — rewrite.
5. Does the first third contain at least one specific proof point from `proof-points.md`? If no — add one or defer consciously.
6. Would a skeptical CTO or revenue lead reading this think "these people understand my business"? If no — cut the generic parts.
7. Am I burning a trope by using it in an asset that doesn't earn it? If yes — pick a different anchor.
8. Have I used ONLY "publishers" as the audience noun when the audience actually includes web apps, games, SaaS, or newsletters? If yes — expand the language (or use "independent web businesses").
9. Is the category framing "ad network" or "plugin" anywhere? If yes — rewrite to **revenue infrastructure / operating layer / infrastructure layer / strategic technology partner**.
10. Am I hedging? ("may," "could," "potentially," "helps to," "allows you to") Where? If a hedge isn't protecting a real legal/factual position, cut it. Ezoic has earned the authority — state the thing.
